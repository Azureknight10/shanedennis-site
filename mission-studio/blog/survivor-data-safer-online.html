How to Keep Survivor Data Safer Online: Practical Tips for Small Teams

By Shane Dennis | Data Privacy & Security

The Burden of Trust

Every time a survivor provides a shelter or advocacy group with their name, location, or history, they are handing over information that, if exposed, could carry catastrophic consequences. Small nonprofits often operate under immense stress and tight budgets, making them prime targets for bad actors or, more commonly, devastating accidental data leaks.

You do not need a million-dollar cyber budget to protect the people you serve. Here are pragmatic, immediate steps your team can take to vastly improve your data posture.

1. Separate PII from Narrative Data

Personally Identifiable Information (PII) includes names, Social Security Numbers, exact dates of birth, and home addresses. Do not store this data in the same spreadsheet or database table as the survivor's narrative history of exploitation.

The Fix: Implement a unique Case ID numbering system. Keep the "Master List" matching Case IDs to real names physically locked away in a safe, or stored in a heavily encrypted, offline-capable digital vault accessed by a single director. Social workers logging daily progress write their case notes attached only to "Case #4229." If the case notes cloud server is breached, the attacker merely finds anonymous stories, not a hit-list of vulnerable people.

2. Mandate Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) Everywhere

A staggering percentage of nonprofit data breaches occur simply because a staff member reused a weak password on their work email. If an attacker gains access to a single inbox, they often gain access to the entire organizational Google Drive or SharePoint.

The Fix: Force MFA (requiring a phone app prompt or text message code in addition to the password) across your entire organization. This applies to emails, case management software, and donor databases. Do not make exceptions for board members or remote volunteers.

3. Stop Emailing Spreadsheets

When staff email Excel sheets titled "Intake_November_Unredacted.xlsx" to collaborative partners, they create an uncontrolled digital footprint. That file now lives on the sender's device, the email server, the recipient's email server, and the recipient's hard drive.

The Fix: Use secure cloud environments with rigid permissions. Share a link to a live document housed securely within your organizational environment, and revoke access when the collaboration concludes. Never transmit raw, unencrypted tables containing sensitive data over standard email protocols.

4. Implement the Rule of Least Privilege

Not everyone in your organization needs to see everything. Does the weekend volunteer coordinating clothing donations need access to the full psychological intake assessments of every client? No.

The Fix: Audit your permissions. Structure your digital files so that team members can only see the specific data required to execute their immediate job function. If they do not need it, they should not be able to read it.

Privacy is Advocacy

Securing data is not just an IT task; it is the fundamental baseline of trauma-informed advocacy. When you prioritize digital privacy, you honor the trust survivors place in your organization.

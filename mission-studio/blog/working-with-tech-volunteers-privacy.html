Working With Tech Volunteers: How to Protect Privacy and Set Clear Boundaries

By Shane Dennis | Operations & Collaboration

The Generosity Dilemma

The tech industry is filled with talented developers, designers, and engineers eager to deploy their skills for a worthy cause. Anti-trafficking nonprofits, constantly operating under immense financial constraints, desperately need those skills.

A partnership seems obvious. Yet, inviting an external volunteer to interact with your organization's digital infrastructure inherently creates tension between necessary progress and absolute security. What happens when a well-meaning volunteer software engineer requests "read access" to your main database to fix a bug in your intake form? How do you ensure survivor names remain strictly confidential while allowing outsiders to modify your tech stack?

At SafeHaven Digital Studio, we function as both the builder and the coordinator for technical projects. Here is how your nonprofit can utilize tech volunteers safely.

1. Provide "Dummy Data", Never Real Production Tables

It is standard for developers to require data to test the software they are building. A common, catastrophic error is handing a volunteer a CSV file containing real client cases, even if "most" of the names are redacted.

The Solution: You must explicitly instruct volunteers to generate totally synthetic, randomized "dummy data" (e.g., using libraries like Faker in Node or Python) to test logic loops, dashboards, or intake forms. Ensure it is established in writing upfront that volunteers will never, under any circumstances, be granted access to the production databases where real survivor PII resides.

2. Establish Granular, Project-Specific Access

Your organization's primary Google Workspace or Microsoft 365 tenant likely holds years of sensitive board minutes, legal strategy documents, and raw intake notes. A volunteer redesigning your public-facing website does not need access to this ecosystem.

The Solution: Spin up isolated environments. If they are redesigning your website, grant them an "Editor" (not Admin) seat solely on the WordPress instance or static CMS. Do not use shared generic passwords (e.g., "admin@shelter.org"). Issue them an explicitly scoped credential tied to their name and revoke it the moment the project concludes.

3. Request NDAs and Zero-Retention Agreements

While an external volunteer may be acting in an entirely pro-bono capacity, your organization holds identical liability for any data they touch. They must be legally bound to your privacy standards.

The Solution: Implement a strong Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA) and explicitly include a "Data Deletion / Zero-Retention" clause. This mandates that the volunteer must irrevocably delete any localized development code, temporary files, or server configurations residing on their personal machines the moment the engagement ends.

4. Assign a Technical "Translator" from Your Staff

Engineers speak in arrays, APIs, and agile sprints. Social workers speak in trauma care, legal precedents, and client stability. Without a translator, an excited volunteer might build a beautiful, complex application that fundamentally fails to serve a traumatized end-user.

The Solution: Appoint a single point of contact within your nonprofit who clearly understands the workflow the new tech is meant to assist. This ensures the volunteer's technical solutions remain rigidly aligned with the organization's trauma-informed ethos.
